Heat Waves

A heat wave is a period of excessively hot weather, which may be accompanied by high humidity, especially in oceanic climate countries.

The deadly heat waves, floods and fires occurring from Japan to the Middle East, and North America to Europe have clear links to human-caused climate change, according to climate scientists, and this summer's abnormal weather is just the beginning of what's in store for us in coming years.

2018 has not been a smooth sailing year for scientists, with the 2018 heat wave puzzling scientists all over the world, and leading to numerous wildfires and droughts across the globe, such as in places like Australia, California, and the United States.

Singapore is heating twice as fast as the rest of the world at 0.25°C per decade. While that might seem minute, Singapore’s maximum daily temperatures could reach 35°C to 37°C by 2100 if carbon emissions continue to rise at the same rate, hence emphasizing on the importance of curbing carbon emissions.

Factors of Global Warming

Therefore, the recent Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) meeting at Incheon, Korea has concluded that “rapid, far-reaching and unprecedented changes in all aspects of society” is required to limit “global warming to 1.5°C” and this would give “clear benefits to people and natural ecosystems in ensuring a more sustainable and equitable society”.

Here’s a brief rundown of what global warming actually constitutes.

The main factor causing global warming and rapid climate change is large concentrations of greenhouse gases being released into the atmosphere, exacerbating the Earth's greenhouse effect.

The sun provides light energy which is absorbed by the Earth and warms the Earth up. The greenhouse effect is the phenomenon whereby greenhouse gases like CO2 trap the outgoing heat from Earth, preventing it from escaping.

The greenhouse effect is vital for survival by keeping the Earth warm, but profligate burning of fossil fuels by humans has led to a large amount of greenhouse gases being released into the atmosphere. Excessive amounts of heat are trapped and re-radiated on Earth, leading to global warming.
Approximately 77% of greenhouse gases are CO2 emissions, as observed from this pie chart. This has also been reiterated in the IPCC Report 2018, that CO2 emissions constitute 78% of greenhouse gas emissions from 2000-2010.

Therefore, the main focus of our proposed Chemistry solution would be to tackle the issue of high CO2 emissions to effectively resolve the pertinent issue of global warming.

Metal-Organic Frameworks (MOFs)

Our proposed solution is Metal Organic Frameworks, abbreviated as MOFs.

But firstly, what exactly are MOFs?

What are MOFs?
This is a MOF.

MOFs are a class of compounds consisting of metal ions or clusters coordinated to organic ligands to form one-, two-, or three-dimensional structures. They are a subclass of coordination polymers, with the special feature that they are often porous.

Put in simple terms, MOFs are crystalline porous structures. They have enormous surface area, high porosity, and depending on the type of MOF, we can vary the pore size and volume.

How are MOFs formed?
MOFs are made from the dative bonding between metal ions, and organic compounds which link the ions together. In dative bonds, the same atom donates 2 electrons, instead of in a conventional covalent bond where electrons are usually shared.

What do MOFs do?

Their enormous surface area, high porosity and variable pore volume render them excellent gas adsorbents.

On a side note... there is a difference between absorption and adsoprtion.
Adsorption means that the specific atoms, ions or molecules simply adhere to the surface of the adsorbent. Whereas absorption is when a specific substance enters the volume of the absorbent or is dissolved by the absorbent.

Some features of MOFs shown in past research papers are that they:

  • show huge potential in adsorption-related experiments
  • have unique structural characteristics and tuneable porosities which are superior over conventional porous materials.
  • have a great availability of configurations, higher porosity & surface area and therefore are expected to be high-capacity adsorbents

How is CO2 adsorbed?

How exactly do MOFs facilitate carbon capture?

We can zoom in on one of the parts of the MOF structure.
And focus on one single 5-coordinate Magnesium cation (5-coordinate i.e. there are 5 organic ligands coordinately bonded to it).
The section of the Magnesium cation which is not sterically hindered is the open metal site.
CO2 can bind to the open metal site through strong van der Waals interactions.

Polarizability of molecule

Polarizability refers to the tendency for a molecule's electron cloud to be distorted, allowing it to form van der Waals' interactions.

The higher the polarizability, the stronger the interactions with the MOF open metal site.

CO2 has higher polarizability than N2 or CH4 as it has the most electrons.

Mg-MOF-74 in CO2 capture

Now, let us see why Mg-MOF-74 is one of the best CO2 adsorbents out there!

As quoted from a research paper, Mg-MOF-74...
has excellent selectivity, easy regeneration, and among the highest dynamic capacities reported for CO2 in porous materials.
Let’s take a look at this graph showing the results from a CO2 gas adsorption experiment.
  • Firstly, Mg-MOF-74 was exposed to a 10mL/min flow of 20% CO2 in CH4. This means 20 percent is CO2, 80% is CH4.
  • After adsorption of CO2 by Mg-MOF-74 took place, the percentage composition of CO2 in the effluent was zero. This shows that there is complete, selective separation of CO2 from CH4.
  • After 5 min, CO2 breakthrough point, where Mg-MOF-74 becomes saturated with CO2, so no more CO2 can be adsorbed.
  • Any extra CO2 begins to pass through the structure.
Clarification

Does this mean that Mg-MOF-74 can only be used for 5 min? No.

Regeneration of Mg-MOF-74

Once Mg-MOF-74 is saturated, purging it with 25 mL/min CH4 at room temperature will restore 87% of its adsorption capacity. Purging at 80°C will allow it to regain its full capacity.

This graph shows us that when the same MOF is regenerated 4 more times, up till cycle 5, its adsorption capacity is still very high.

We also have to take into account the mass of Mg-MOF-74 used during the researchers' experiment.

As quoted from the same research paper's Appendix Section S5:
In a representative breakthrough experiment, 191.4 mg Mg-MOF-74 were packed between cotton plugs in the sample cell with stainless steel beads as necessary to fill the volume.
The research paper's results showed that:
...we calculate that Mg-MOF-74 takes up 8.9 wt % CO2 before breakthrough, corresponding to 0.44 molecules of CO2 per magnesium ion. This equates to a volumetric capacity of 81 g of CO2 per liter of adsorbent based on a calculated density of 0.91109 g mL-1.

Hence we can perform the calculations below:

$$ \begin{align*}\text{Mass of Mg-MOF-74}\;=&\;191.4\times10^{-3}\ \text{g}\\\text{Density of Mg-MOF-74}\;=&\;0.91109\ \text{gmL}^{-1}\\\text{Volume of Mg-MOF-74}\;=&\;\frac{191.4\times10^{-3}}{0.91109}\ \text{mL}\\\;=&\;2.100780384\times10^{-1}\ \text{mL}\\\;=&\;2.100780384\times10^{-4}\ \text{L}\\\text{Volumetric Capacity}\;=&\;81\ \text{g of CO}_2\ \text{ per L}\\\therefore\text{CO}_2\ \text{adsorbed}=&\;81\times2.100780384\times10^{-4}\ \text{g}\\\;=&\;1.70163211\times10^{-2}\ \text{g}\\\;=&\;1.70163211\ \times10^{-5}\ \text{kg}\end{align*} $$

Since this measurement is in units of mass, we must convert it to volume in order for it to be meaningful data. Given that standard temperature and pressure (s.t.p.) refers to standard temperature (0°C) and standard pressure (100kPa):

$$ \begin{align*}\text{Density of CO}_2\text{ at s.t.p.}\;=&\;1.977\ \text{kgm}^{-3}\\\text{Mass of CO}_2\text{ adsorbed}\;=&\;1.70163211\times10^{-5}\ \text{kg}\\\text{Volume of CO}_2\text{ adsorbed}\;=&\;\frac{1.70163211\times10^{-5}}{1.977}\ \text{m}^3\\\;=&\;8.60714270\times10^{-6}\ \text{m}^3\\\;=&\;8.60714270\ \text{mL}\\\;\approx&\;8.6\ \text{mL}\end{align*} $$

This is the volume of CO2 that 191.4 mg of Mg-MOF-74 can take up. Now, what if we scale this up to 1 kg?

$$ \begin{align*}\text{Volume of CO}_2\text{ adsorbed by 1kg Mg-MOF-74}\;=&\;\frac{1\times10^3}{191.4\times10^{-3}}\times8.60714270\ \text{mL}\\\;=&\;4.49693976\ \text{L}\\\;\approx&\;4.5\ \text{L}\end{align*} $$

From this calculation, we can conclude that if flow rate is kept identical at 10 mL min-1, a medical-grade gas canister (10.16cm by 66.04cm) could be filled up in 5 min.

There is another problem with the current method to liberate the stored CO2. We started out separating a 10 mL min-1 mix of 20% CO2 and 80% CH4. However, we are using CH4 to release the captured CO2, which may seem counter-intuitive as we are going back to square one.

This begs the question: How could we release the captured CO2 without purging it with the same gas we are separating it from?

Fortunately, there are enhancements we could make to the Mg-MOF-74 such that this problem is rectified.

Introducing LISA, MISA and MaLISA

The solution to the problem is LISA, MISA and MaLISA.

LISA stands for Light Induction Swing Adsorption, MISA stands for Magnetic Induction Swing Adsorption, and MaLISA is a combination of LISA and MISA.

Comparison against existing solutions

Having learnt about the effectiveness of Mg-MOF-74, why then should we use MOFs for carbon capture instead of other current existing solutions?

Amine solutions

A widely used adsorbent in industries nowadays is amine solutions. They are solvents that comprise water and amines (NH2), which undergo complex reversible reactions with acidic gases such as CO2.

When CO2 is passed into amine solutions, it binds to the NH2 groups and is removed from the gas. The card below outlines the detailed mechanism of action for one type of amine solution.

However, a problem with amine solutions is its regeneration. Regenerating the amine solutions requires diverting high temperature steam that would normally be effective in making electricity in the power plant.

Instead, if we use MOFs, which have milder regeneration conditions (room temperature and 87°C as seen above), the high temperature steam need not be diverted. This allows the steam to be used effectively for generation of electricity.

Zeolites

Another CO2 adsorbent is zeolites. They are are crystalline solid structures made of silicon, aluminum and oxygen that form a framework with cavities and channels inside where cations, water and/or small molecules may reside.

However, zeolites’ pores are confined by rigid skeletons that are usually difficult to alter.

Instead, structures and properties of MOFs can be well-designed and systematically tuned by the choice of metal-based building blocks and organic linkers. Hence, it can be applied to many scenarios. Such as for adsorbing gas in different pressures, different temperatures, etc.
